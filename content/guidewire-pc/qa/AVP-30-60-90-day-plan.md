# 30-60-90 Day Plan — HM Interview Prep

**Why this section exists:** Hiring managers at the AVP level often close interviews with: "Walk me through what your first 90 days would look like." This question has multiple layers — they want to see that you can think structurally, prioritize intelligently, and understand what matters in this specific role. A generic answer fails. A specific, phased plan that reflects VM's business wins.

---

## The Question

**"If you joined ValueMomentum as AVP of Client Services next month, what would your first 90 days look like?"**

---

## How to Answer: Full Narrative Version

> "I'd approach the first 90 days in three distinct phases — not because it's a clean framework, but because the work genuinely changes as you go from listening to executing to leading.
>
> **Days 1–30: Listen, map, and don't break anything.**
>
> The first 30 days are about building an accurate picture — not acting on assumptions I brought in from EY. I'd start with a structured listening tour across five groups:
>
> First, the active client portfolio. I'd join every ongoing engagement's weekly status call in observer mode and do a one-on-one with each delivery manager. I want to understand: where are the risks, who are the key relationships, and where is the work that I need to pay attention to in the next 60 days.
>
> Second, the VM Guidewire practice leadership. I'd spend time with the practice head, the pre-sales team, and the solution architects to understand: what capabilities we have, what's in the pipeline, and where the delivery gaps are against what we're selling.
>
> Third, my team. I'd do 1:1s with every direct report — delivery managers, leads — focused on: what's working, what's not, and what would they fix if they were in my seat. People tell you the truth in the first 30 days if you ask the right questions.
>
> Fourth, key carrier contacts. I'd ask VM leadership for introductions to the client-side sponsors on our three most strategic accounts. Not to change anything — just to introduce myself, ask about the relationship, and signal continuity.
>
> Fifth, VM leadership. I'd have a direct conversation with the CEO and VP level on VM's 12-month priorities, where the Guidewire practice fits in the growth plan, and what success looks like for this role after year one.
>
> By day 30, I want a clear picture of: the health of every active engagement, the state of the pipeline, the talent gaps in my team, and the two or three things that need immediate attention.
>
> **Days 31–60: Execute on the urgent, establish the operating model.**
>
> This is where I move from listening to acting on what I've learned.
>
> I'd establish a weekly operating cadence: a portfolio health review with my delivery managers (30 minutes, RAG format, decisions-focused), a monthly P&L review with VM finance, and a pipeline alignment meeting with pre-sales to keep delivery capacity and sales pipeline synchronized.
>
> I'd address the most urgent delivery risks first. If I found engagements in the listening tour that were at risk — scope drift, team gaps, client tension — those get managed directly. I don't let month-old problems become month-two crises.
>
> I'd start building the practice infrastructure: a skills and certification matrix across my team, a structured onboarding plan for the next three hires, and a review of what IP already exists in iFoundry and what's missing. This isn't a strategy document — it's an operating baseline.
>
> I'd also identify one visible win I can drive in this period. It might be closing a contract renewal, resolving a client escalation that had been stuck, or landing a new SOW on an existing account. Something that signals to VM leadership and to my team that I'm contributing, not just observing.
>
> **Days 61–90: Set the direction, build the forward plan.**
>
> By day 60, I have enough information to stop listening and start leading.
>
> I'd put together a 12-month practice plan — specific, not aspirational. It would cover: the three or four accounts I want to grow and how, the two capabilities I want to invest in (likely cloud migration depth and GenAI delivery), the certification roadmap for my team, and the talent additions I'd recommend.
>
> I'd present this plan to VM leadership — not as a report, but as a conversation. Here's what I've found, here's what I'm going to do about it, here's what I need from you. I'd want explicit alignment on priorities before I execute.
>
> I'd also have a client-facing deliverable by day 90: either a thought leadership POV, a carrier advisory session, or a QBR with a strategic account where I'm present and visible. Something that signals VM has a new AVP who is engaged and forward-looking.
>
> The measure of success at 90 days: every active engagement is healthy or on a managed recovery plan, my team knows my operating style, and VM leadership can see a clear plan for the year ahead."

## Condensed Version (2–3 minutes)

> "First 30 days — I listen before I act. Structured listening tour: my delivery managers, the active client portfolio, VM practice leadership, and introductions to key carrier contacts. My goal is an accurate picture of portfolio health, team capability, and pipeline — not acting on EY assumptions.
>
> Days 31–60 — I execute on the urgent and establish my operating model. I address whatever risks surfaced in the first 30 days, set up a weekly portfolio health review cadence, and start building the practice infrastructure: skills matrix, certification roadmap, IP inventory.
>
> Days 61–90 — I shift from learning to leading. I develop a 12-month practice plan — specific accounts to grow, capabilities to build, talent to add — and present it to VM leadership for alignment. And I make sure I have one visible, client-facing win by day 90 that signals I'm contributing, not just observing."

## Q2: What would you prioritize if you found — in your first 30 days — that two of the active engagements were at serious risk?

**What they're really asking:** Crisis triage. What's your instinct when you walk into a burning building?

**How to answer:**

> "My first move is triage, not panic. Two at-risk engagements simultaneously means I need to understand which one is closer to a cliff and which has more runway.
>
> I'd spend 2 hours with each delivery manager doing a structured risk review: what's the specific risk, what's the timeline to impact, what's already been tried, and what does the client know? That last question matters — if the client doesn't know there's a risk, my intervention plan looks very different than if they're already aware.
>
> Then I make a ruthless prioritization call. I can't give both engagements 100% of my attention simultaneously. I identify the one that's closer to client escalation or go-live failure and I go there personally — I don't delegate the hard one. I take point on the client relationship and put my most experienced delivery resource on the second.
>
> At EY, I walked into a situation at Alfa Insurance where the prior DM had left mid-program. Two workstreams were at risk: data migration and billing integration. I couldn't fully rescue both simultaneously with the team on the ground. I made a call: data migration was 6 weeks from SIT — that was the crisis. Billing integration had a 10-week buffer. I focused on data migration directly, restructured the sprint plan, and moved two offshore resources from billing to migration for 3 weeks. We hit our SIT date. The billing workstream extended by 2 weeks, which we disclosed proactively to the carrier. Client accepted it without escalation because we told them before it happened.
>
> The lesson: clear prioritization and proactive communication prevent two bad situations from becoming one catastrophic one."

## Q3: How would you handle the tension between ramping into the role and being expected to contribute to deals in the pipeline immediately?

**What they're really asking:** Self-awareness about the dual mandate of a new AVP: learn the business while generating revenue.

**How to answer:**

> "There's no tension if you structure it right. Pre-sales in the first 30 days is actually a learning opportunity, not a distraction — sitting in a carrier discovery call tells me more about VM's delivery approach and the client's real needs than any internal briefing.
>
> My ask to VM leadership: include me in pipeline conversations from day one, even as an observer. Let me see the deals you're working, the proposals you're writing, the carrier discovery calls you're running. I'll contribute where I have relevant insight — if a deal is in workers comp and I ran the CompSource Mutual program, I have something to add. I'll stay quiet where I need more context.
>
> What I won't do is lead a deal pitch in week 2 without understanding VM's commercial model, pricing philosophy, or how the delivery team is structured. That's a setup for saying the wrong thing in front of a carrier.
>
> By week 6 or 8, I'd expect to be leading sections of proposals and joining carrier discovery calls as the Guidewire delivery lead. By month 3, I should be able to run a full pre-sales cycle independently.
>
> The way I think about it: the best thing I can do for VM's pipeline in the first 90 days is learn fast enough that when I'm in the room with a carrier, I represent VM credibly — not just represent myself."

## KEY PHRASES FOR THIS QUESTION

- "First 30 days: I listen before I act — I've got assumptions from EY that may or may not apply here."
- "My goal at day 30 is an accurate picture of portfolio health, team capability, and what needs immediate attention."
- "By day 90, every active engagement is healthy or on a managed recovery plan, and VM leadership has seen a 12-month practice plan from me."
- "I want one visible win by day 90 — something that shows I'm contributing, not just ramping."
- "I don't lead a deal I don't understand yet — but I do want to be in the room from day one."
