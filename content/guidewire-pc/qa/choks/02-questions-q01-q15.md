## SECTION A: LEADERSHIP & STRATEGIC

**Q1. How do you define success in a client services leadership role?**

> "Three things: delivery outcomes, client health, and team retention. A program that goes live on time but leaves the client relationship damaged is not a success. A client relationship that's warm but the delivery is late is not a success either. And if I'm burning out my team to hit both, that's not sustainable. I measure success by: are my clients renewing and expanding? Are my deliveries on-time and within budget? Are my people growing and staying? If all three are green, I'm doing my job."

> **Deep detail — Guidewire-specific example (STAR):**
> *Situation:* At Nationwide Personal Lines, 2 months post go-live on PolicyCenter, the business team was still relying on workarounds for endorsements.
> *Task:* Success for me wasn't the go-live — it was the carrier operating independently.
> *Action:* I ran weekly enablement sessions with the carrier's product team on PolicyCenter configuration, stayed through hypercare, and handed over a documented runbook.
> *Result:* Within 60 days of go-live, the carrier's product team launched a new homeowners endorsement in PolicyCenter with zero escalation to us. That self-sufficiency moment was the real success signal.
>
> The commercial lens: I target net revenue retention above 110% — clients who renew AND expand signal true value delivery. The ultimate marker is when a client moves from satisfied to advocate — when they're introducing us to other carriers in their network. That's the top of the success ladder.

**Q2. Describe your approach to building and scaling a client services practice.**

> "I build on three pillars: a structured delivery model, reusable IP, and people development. First, define the delivery model — team structure for a typical engagement, onshore vs. offshore split, standard roles. Second, build reusable IP — estimation templates, RACI frameworks, integration patterns, QA checklists. Third, develop the people — structured learning tied to real engagement work, not classroom theory. I run certification tracks tied to the same milestone discipline we use for delivery. Once you have a repeatable model and practitioners who keep growing, you can scale without quality degrading."

> **Deep detail — scaling example:**
> When I scaled from managing 8 Guidewire clients to 22 across personal auto, homeowners, and workers' comp, I segmented the book by complexity. Carriers mid-migration — active PolicyCenter or ClaimCenter implementations — required dedicated weekly steering and a dedicated Client Partner for accounts above $5M ARR. Post-go-live clients moved to a quarterly QBR cadence with pooled resources and automated health scoring. Smaller accounts moved to a digital-first model. That segmentation reduced unplanned escalations by 40% because the right level of attention was matched to the right account stage. The key insight: a post-go-live carrier needs adoption support, not implementation governance — those are different service models.

**Q4. How do you balance short-term client firefighting with long-term relationship development?**

> "You can't do both simultaneously — so you build structure that protects each from the other. I establish a Delivery Lead as the day-to-day escalation owner. P1 and P2 issues don't land on me — they land on the delivery lead who surfaces only what needs my executive intervention. That frees at least 40% of my client-facing time for strategic conversations: roadmap alignment, exec relationship building, identifying the next problem before it becomes an RFP. When those lines blur — when I'm personally resolving every integration defect — I've lost the strategic layer entirely."

> **Deep detail — turning a fire into a strategy conversation:**
> During a live ClaimCenter rollout for a personal auto carrier, we hit a data migration defect that blocked adjuster workflows for 48 hours. I activated the incident bridge and explicitly delegated recovery to the PM — his problem to own and resolve. I used that window to schedule a direct executive debrief with the carrier's COO. What could have been a damage-control conversation became a roadmap discussion: how do we prevent this class of defect in future phases, what does a long-term claims transformation strategy look like. The carrier's COO later told me that's the conversation that shifted how he thought about us — from a vendor managing incidents to a partner thinking ahead. The 40% strategic time rule isn't aspirational — it's a discipline I enforce structurally by not being available for operational issues.

**Q5. What's your philosophy on building and retaining high-performing client services teams?**

> "Clarity, growth, and recognition. Clarity: every person knows exactly what they're accountable for and how it connects to the client outcome. Growth: I protected 10% of team time for skill-building at EY — not lip service, actually tracked. And I created real visibility for junior team members: running client workshops, presenting in steering committees, leading architecture reviews. Recognition: I make it specific and public. When a developer solves a hard problem, the carrier's CIO hears that person's name. On retention: I run stay interviews quarterly — not exit interviews. > **Deep detail — Domain Depth program (attrition 28%→11%):**
> On a team of 12 CSMs across personal lines Guidewire accounts, attrition was running at 28% annually — typical for implementation consulting but not acceptable when institutional knowledge walks out with every departure. I introduced a structured Domain Depth program: every CSM on a Guidewire account completed a structured learning track within 90 days of joining, covering the platform we were delivering (PolicyCenter product model, ClaimCenter claim lifecycle, or BillingCenter billing plans). Beyond certification, CSMs shadowed Guidewire implementation workshops — learning alongside carrier business analysts, not just delivering to them. That exposure meant CSMs could hold substantive conversations with carriers about product configuration, not just status reporting. Within a year, attrition dropped from 28% to 11%. The reason: people felt they were building a career skill set, not just executing tasks. I also built a defined career ladder — Associate CSM → CSM → Senior CSM → Client Partner — so every person could see where they were going.

## SECTION B: CLIENT RELATIONSHIP & ACCOUNT MANAGEMENT

**Q6. Walk me through how you've managed a large, complex client account. How did you structure the engagement?**

> "Nationwide Personal Lines — 18-month PolicyCenter and BillingCenter implementation, personal auto and personal property, 50+ team across three workstreams. Three layers: delivery layer (Dev, QA, Integration leads, weekly deliverable commitments), client layer (I owned the IT VP and business sponsor — weekly steering, biweekly exec summary), and commercial layer (P&L tracked monthly — utilization, margin, scope change log, renewal status). The principle: the right people making the right decisions at the right level. My job was protecting the team from client noise and protecting the client from internal delivery issues."

> **Deep detail — full suite implementation governance:**
> On a $6M annual engagement implementing Guidewire PolicyCenter, BillingCenter, and ClaimCenter simultaneously — an 18-month program with 4 system integrations and a full data migration from a legacy mainframe — I ran a monthly SteerCo with the carrier's CIO, CFO, and Chief Claims Officer. Not just IT leadership — finance and operations were at the table from day one because a suite implementation affects all three domains: policy (IT/business), billing (finance/CFO), and claims (operations/COO).
>
> Workstream separation was the most critical governance decision. Each of the three suites had its own delivery lead, its own sprint cadence, and its own integration test track. Cross-workstream dependencies were surfaced weekly in a dependency review I ran personally. By maintaining that separation, we avoided the scope bleed that typically derails suite implementations. PolicyCenter went live on schedule. BillingCenter followed 6 weeks later. The carrier told us it was the first time in their history they'd delivered a major IT program on time. That outcome didn't happen by accident — it happened because the governance structure matched the complexity of what we were delivering.

**Q9. How do you handle a situation where a client's expectations are misaligned with what delivery can realistically offer?**

I always offer three paths: descope to stay on timeline, extend timeline to include the scope, or fund additional resources. Never deliver a problem without a path forward. I didn't say 'we can't hit your date.' I said 'here's the date I can commit to, here's what's driving it, here's what I'd recommend you tell your board, and here's what I'm accountable for.'"

> **Deep detail — Alfa turnaround + WC NCCI example:**
> At Alfa Insurance, inheriting a mid-flight program, the client expected a go-live in 6 weeks. My assessment showed 14 weeks of real work remaining. I requested a 1:1 with the CIO — just the two of us — and came with the data: backlog velocity, open defect count, integration items still unstarted. He pushed back. I didn't move off the number. "I understand what this means for your board commitment. Here's what I'd recommend you tell them, and here's what I'm committing to you in return." He accepted it. The date held.
>
> WC-specific example: A workers' comp carrier expected their PolicyCenter implementation to include automated NCCI classification code validation — comparing submitted class codes against NCCI's published schedules to flag misclassified WC exposures. This was not in scope. Rather than letting the expectation fester, I presented a formal scope change order within one week: $180K, 6-week timeline, clear deliverable. Not to criticize the competitor — because I need to know what they're afraid of. The fear usually comes from one of three things: missed commitments, communication gaps, or a feeling of being sold to rather than served. Then I set up explicit rituals that address those fears directly. If they were burned by missed commitments: weekly RAG milestone tracking, nothing stays yellow more than 48 hours without a remediation plan. Trust is rebuilt through consistency, not promises. 90 days of steady delivery before a skeptical client starts relaxing."

> **Deep detail — four-stage trust recovery model:**
> I use a deliberate four-stage model with damaged clients:
> - **Week 1 — Clean Slate session:** I run a structured listening session: what went wrong, what they need to see differently from us. No defensiveness, just diagnosis. The act of asking — and listening without deflecting — already starts to differentiate.
> - **Month 1 — Over-communicate:** I send status updates before they ask. Every milestone, every dependency, every risk — they hear it from me first. A client who has been burned by surprises needs to experience a vendor who never surprises them.
> - **Month 2–3 — Quick win:** I identify something visible and valued that I can deliver ahead of expectation. Something they can point to internally as proof the relationship is different.
> - **Ongoing:** I never deliver bad news without a solution attached. Problems come with remediation plans.
>
> Homeowners carrier example: came to us after a prior SI botched a PolicyCenter migration, leaving 40,000 policies in a broken state. Before any contract conversation, I commissioned a free 2-week data quality assessment by our migration team and presented the findings with a complete remediation plan — at no charge. That investment before the contract was signed rebuilt confidence faster than any SLA language. The carrier told us afterward: "You were the only firm that showed us the problem before asking us to sign."

**Q11. What metrics do you use to measure client satisfaction and health?**

> "Five categories, weighted and scored quarterly. Delivery health (25%): on-time milestones, open P1/P2 defects, SLA attainment in Managed Services. Relationship health (20%): NPS equivalent, exec sponsor accessibility, escalation frequency. Commercial health (25%): renewal probability, active expansion pipeline, invoice aging. Adoption health (20%): system utilization, STP rates, training completion. Strategic alignment (10%): roadmap sessions completed, joint planning participation. Everything rolls into a single-page account health scorecard — green/yellow/red by category. If anything goes yellow, we have a remediation conversation before the client notices."

> **Deep detail — adoption health in practice:**
> Adoption health is the category most teams skip — and it's where post-go-live relationships actually break down. On a personal lines carrier post-PolicyCenter go-live, I tracked adjuster adoption by monitoring two specific metrics: PolicyCenter login frequency by user role, and policy issuance straight-through processing (STP) rate. When STP dropped below 70% in month 2 — meaning 30%+ of new policy submissions required manual intervention — I proactively scheduled targeted re-training on the commercial auto module. The carrier hadn't noticed yet. We resolved it before their internal KPI reports surfaced it at the next QBR.
>
> The structural discipline: I share this scorecard with the engagement partner and my delivery lead every month. Not selectively — all five categories, all accounts. That forces a conversation about anything trending yellow before it becomes a client escalation. Commercial health signals (invoice aging, renewal probability) are particularly important because they give me 60–90 days of runway to course-correct before a contract decision point.
