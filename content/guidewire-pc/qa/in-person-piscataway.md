#### B6. "How do you handle a client who is unhappy with your team's performance?" **[ANSWER BELOW]**

> "I separate the emotion from the facts first. When a client is unhappy, there's a real underlying problem — and often a communication failure on top of it.
>
> My first step is always a direct call — not intermediaries, not email. I hear it from the client in their own words. I listen without defending. Then I diagnose: delivery issue, communication issue, or expectation gap?
>
> Each has a different fix. Delivery issue: immediate action, timeline, follow-up with the result. Communication issue: change the cadence, the format, or the person who owns the touchpoint. Expectation gap: a harder conversation about what was agreed versus what is realistic — I don't let that fester.
>
> My rule: an unhappy client doesn't stay unhappy for more than 48 hours without a direct response from me. The worst thing I can do is let silence compound a problem that already has heat on it."

#### B8. "How do you manage teams across onshore and offshore locations?" **[ANSWER BELOW]**

> "Structure and rhythm. Without both, distributed teams drift.
>
> Structure: I define clear workstream ownership — each offshore team has a specific senior lead onshore who owns the handoff, the code review gate, and the daily sync. I don't run a model where everything goes through a single PM. Ownership has to be close to the work.
>
> Rhythm: I run a 30-minute daily standup across all workstreams — it's decisions-focused, not status. If something needs a longer conversation, it gets a separate call within the same day. I also run a weekly cross-team dependency review — this is where I catch integration conflicts before they become sprint blockers.
>
> The thing that kills offshore delivery is timezone-delay on decisions. If an offshore developer is blocked at 9 AM India time and the answer doesn't come until 3 PM their time — you've lost half a day. I give my offshore leads decision authority for defined categories so they don't wait for me on things they can resolve themselves.
>
> On the Nationwide program, 70% of the team was offshore. We ran two-week sprints with daily touchpoints and a documented decision log so the offshore team always knew where decisions stood. We hit every sprint commitment in the last 12 sprints of the program."

#### B9. "How do you handle a situation where two of your delivery managers disagree on how to handle a client issue?" **[ANSWER BELOW]**

> "I let them make the argument to me, not to each other. If two delivery managers are stuck in a disagreement, the resolution doesn't happen by letting them debate — it happens by getting them aligned on which outcome we're optimizing for.
>
> My approach: I bring them to a common frame. Here is what the client needs, here is what the program needs, here is the constraint we're operating under. Given those three things, what does each of you recommend? Usually the disagreement isn't about facts — it's about which priority wins. Once I name the priority, the path becomes clearer.
>
> I make the call if needed. I don't let disagreements linger in delivery — indecision in a delivery team shows up in the client relationship within two weeks.
>
> And I follow up privately with both afterward — not to re-litigate the decision, but to make sure neither person walks away feeling dismissed. I need both of them fully committed to the path forward, not silently resistant."
