## SECTION 1: Leadership & Strategic Questions

**Q1. How do you define success in a client services leadership role?**

Success is defined across three dimensions: client health, commercial growth, and team excellence. It starts with retention — if clients renew and expand, we're delivering value. It extends to net revenue retention above 110%, measured through upsell and cross-sell, and culminates in clients becoming advocates who refer us to peers.

*Guidewire Example:* At a prior engagement, managing a regional personal lines carrier through a Guidewire PolicyCenter implementation. Success wasn't just go-live — it was when the carrier's product team independently launched a new homeowners endorsement in PolicyCenter within 60 days of go-live, with zero escalation. That self-sufficiency moment signaled true success: the client owned the outcome.

---

**Q2. Describe your approach to building and scaling a client services practice.**

Built on three pillars: structured engagement models, proactive governance, and a talent pipeline. First, establish tiered account management — strategic accounts (over $5M ARR) get a dedicated Client Partner, mid-market accounts get pooled resources with quarterly business reviews, and smaller accounts get a digital-first model with automated health scoring.

Second, implement a Client Health Dashboard that tracks delivery milestones, ticket SLAs, CSAT scores, and commercial signals. Third, invest in career pathing for CSMs so the practice retains institutional knowledge.

*P&C Example:* When scaling from managing 8 Guidewire clients to 22 across personal auto, homeowners, and workers' comp, segmented the book by complexity — carriers mid-migration (active PolicyCenter or ClaimCenter implementations) required dedicated weekly steering, while post-go-live clients moved to a quarterly cadence. This reduced unplanned escalations by 40%.

---

**Q3. How have you contributed to the strategic growth of accounts you've managed? What was your methodology?**

Methodology is Account-Based Growth Planning — each strategic account gets an annual growth plan co-created with the client that maps their 3-year roadmap to EY's service offerings. Run joint planning sessions with the client's CIO and business owners to uncover whitespace.

- Map client's strategic initiatives (digital transformation, cloud migration) to EY capabilities
- Identify adoption gaps in current engagement (e.g., client using PolicyCenter but not BillingCenter)
- Build an 18-month commercial pipeline within the account

*Real Example:* A workers' comp carrier was live on Guidewire PolicyCenter but managing billing manually. Through a joint planning session, identified a path to Guidewire BillingCenter implementation — a $2.4M expansion. Positioned EY as the implementation partner by showcasing BillingCenter accelerators for WC premium calculations and installment billing. That deal closed within 6 months of the planning session.

**Q4. How do you balance short-term client firefighting with long-term relationship development?**

Dual-track model: separate operational management from strategic engagement and protect time for both. Fires get triaged and delegated — establish a Delivery Lead as the day-to-day escalation point so bandwidth isn't consumed by every integration defect. Step in at executive escalation level, not at ticket level.

Protect a minimum of 40% of client-facing time for strategic conversations — roadmap alignment, exec relationship building, and identifying emerging needs before they become problems.

*Guidewire Example:* During a live Guidewire ClaimCenter rollout for a personal auto carrier, hit a data migration defect that temporarily blocked adjuster workflows for 48 hours. Activated the incident bridge, let the PM manage recovery, and used the crisis as an opportunity to schedule an executive debrief with the carrier's COO — turning a fire into a relationship-deepening conversation about long-term claims transformation strategy.

---

**Q5. What's your philosophy on building and retaining high-performing client services teams?**

Philosophy: hire for curiosity and empathy, develop for domain depth, and retain through ownership. The best CSMs in the insurance tech space aren't just relationship managers — they understand P&C business processes well enough to challenge client assumptions and propose better solutions.

- Hire people who ask 'why' — they uncover client needs others miss
- Build structured domain enablement — every CSM on Guidewire accounts goes through PolicyCenter and ClaimCenter product training within 90 days
- Create internal career ladders: Associate CSM → CSM → Sr. CSM → Client Partner
- Recognize client impact publicly — 'deal expansion' wins shared in team all-hands

*Retention Lever:* When managing a team of 12 CSMs across personal lines Guidewire accounts, introduced a 'Domain Depth' program where CSMs shadowed Guidewire implementation workshops. Attrition dropped from 28% to 11% in one year because the team felt they were growing, not just managing.

**Q8. How do you identify upsell and cross-sell opportunities without being perceived as pushy?**

The key is timing and framing. Never present an upsell in isolation — it always emerges from a client pain or aspiration the client has already articulated. 'Anchoring to their words.' If a carrier's VP of Operations says 'our billing exceptions are out of control,' don't pitch BillingCenter the next day — schedule a working session to understand the billing workflow, then present a business case that maps their stated problem to a solution.

*Cross-sell Example:* A personal auto carrier was live on Guidewire PolicyCenter. In a QBR, the Chief Actuary mentioned their loss ratio was trending above plan and they couldn't isolate causal factors fast enough. Recognized this as a data/analytics gap — not a core Guidewire gap. Introduced EY's analytics practice and proposed a ClaimCenter implementation that would give them structured claims data feeding their actuarial models. The conversation started with their loss ratio pain, not with our product catalog.

**Q9. How do you handle a situation where a client's expectations are misaligned with what delivery can realistically offer?**

Address misalignment early, directly, and with data — never hoping it will self-correct. The moment expectation drift is detected (usually through informal client signals before formal escalation), initiate a 'Scope Alignment Review.'

- Pull the original SOW and map current delivery status against committed scope
- Present the gap analysis to the client's project sponsor in a working session — not via email
- Offer three paths: descope to stay on timeline, extend timeline to include the additional scope, or fund additional resources

*WC Example:* A workers' comp carrier expected their Guidewire PolicyCenter implementation to include automated NCCI classification code validation — this was not in scope. Rather than letting the expectation fester, presented a scope change order within a week of identifying the misalignment, with a clear $180K cost and 6-week timeline estimate. The client approved the change order because we came with data and options, not defensiveness.

**Q10. How do you build trust with a client who has had a bad prior experience with a vendor?**

Trust with a damaged client is built through consistency over time, not through promises at the outset. First move: listen without defending — understand specifically what went wrong with the prior vendor.

- Week 1: Structured 'Clean Slate' session — what went wrong, what they need to see differently
- Month 1: Over-communicate on delivery — send status updates before they ask
- Month 2-3: Deliver one meaningful 'quick win' — something visible and valued
- Ongoing: Never surprise them with bad news; bring problems with solutions attached

*Personal Lines Example:* A homeowners carrier came to EY after a failed Guidewire PolicyCenter implementation with a prior SI. Their data migration had been botched, leaving 40,000 policies in a broken state. First action: commissioned a free 2-week data quality assessment by our migration team and presented the findings with a remediation plan — no charge. That gesture of investing before the contract was signed rebuilt confidence faster than any SLA commitment.

**Q11. What metrics do you use to measure client satisfaction and health?**

Multi-dimensional Client Health Score across 5 categories, scored quarterly:
- Delivery Health (25%): On-time milestones, open critical defects, SLA compliance on support tickets
- Relationship Health (20%): Exec sponsor accessibility, stakeholder NPS, escalation frequency
- Commercial Health (25%): On-time payments, contract renewal probability, active upsell pipeline
- Adoption Health (20%): System utilization rates, user-reported issues, training completion
- Strategic Alignment (10%): Roadmap alignment sessions completed, joint planning participation

*Guidewire Example:* For a personal lines carrier post-PolicyCenter go-live, tracked adjuster adoption by monitoring PolicyCenter login frequency and policy issuance STP (straight-through processing) rates. When STP dropped below 70% in month 2, proactively scheduled targeted re-training — catching the adoption issue before it became a client complaint.

## SECTION 4: Delivery & Escalation Management

**Q16. Describe a major delivery escalation you handled. What was the root cause, and what did you do?**

A personal lines carrier was 14 months into a Guidewire ClaimCenter implementation for auto and homeowners when the carrier's CIO escalated to EY's leadership. The system wasn't ready for UAT and go-live was 3 months out. The client was threatening to invoke contract penalties.

Root cause: (1) The integration with the carrier's payment processor had 3x more exception scenarios than scoped, and (2) a key carrier SME had left mid-project, creating a knowledge vacuum in the claims configuration workstream.

Actions:
- Immediate: Activated a 2-day war room with EY delivery lead, integration architect, and the carrier's IT VP to triage the backlog and separate blocker issues from cosmetic items
- Short-term: Brought in a EY senior ClaimCenter architect for a 6-week intensive — off standard billing rate — to accelerate the integration defect resolution
- Structural: Introduced a biweekly 'Delivery Health Check' between EY's delivery director and the carrier's PMO — a governance layer that had been missing

*Outcome:* UAT started 5 weeks late but the go-live was delayed by only 3 weeks versus the original plan. The carrier withdrew the penalty clause. The CIO told EY's CEO that the way we responded to the crisis was more impressive than anything we'd done during normal delivery.

**Q17. How do you work with delivery/project teams to ensure client commitments are met?**

Position as the client-facing accountability layer, not a delivery manager. Job is to ensure delivery teams have context on what matters most to the client — and that client commitments are never made without delivery team input.

- Pre-commitment: Involve the delivery lead in any conversation where a client milestone, deadline, or scope is being discussed. No commitments made on the fly.
- Weekly: Attend (but don't run) the delivery status meeting to stay calibrated — need to know what's amber before the client does
- Escalation protocol: Clear RACI — delivery PM owns day-to-day issues, I own client executive communication on anything Red

*Guidewire Example:* For a workers' comp carrier on a Guidewire PolicyCenter and BillingCenter implementation, introduced a shared 'Commitment Register' — a live document listing every commitment made to the carrier, by whom, and by when. The delivery PM updated it weekly. This eliminated the all-too-common gap where the CSM promises something the delivery team doesn't know was promised.

---

**Q18. How do you handle scope creep when a client keeps adding requests outside the contract?**

Approach: never saying 'no' — but always saying 'yes, and here's what that means.'

- Prevention: SOW language is explicit about what constitutes a change request vs. a defect vs. in-scope configuration. Walk clients through this at kickoff.
- Detection: Train delivery PMs to flag any out-of-scope request within 24 hours — not to block it, but to size it
- Response: For every out-of-scope request, prepare a 1-page Change Impact Summary showing effort, cost, and timeline impact — then present options

*WC Example:* A workers' comp carrier on a Guidewire PolicyCenter implementation asked mid-project to add e-signature integration for policy issuance — not in scope. Rather than a hard 'no,' presented three paths: (1) add to current program for $85K/6 weeks; (2) defer to Phase 2 post-go-live; (3) EY provides specifications so the carrier's internal team builds it. The client chose option 2 — and we booked a Phase 2 SOW. Scope creep became a pipeline event.

**Q19. What processes do you put in place to proactively catch issues before clients raise them?**

'Client Early Warning System' across three signals:
- Delivery signals: Weekly automated report on open critical/high tickets, milestone slippage, and integration test failure rates — if any metric moves to amber, review before the client's weekly call
- Relationship signals: Informal pulse calls with mid-level client stakeholders (not just the sponsor) — they often know about brewing frustrations before leadership escalates
- Commercial signals: Invoice aging, renewal date proximity, budget cycle timing — a carrier that's 45 days from budget planning needs a value realization conversation now

*Guidewire ClaimCenter Example:* For a personal auto carrier post-ClaimCenter go-live, implemented a monthly 'System Health Report' — pulling adjuster login rates, average claim cycle time, and STP %. When cycle time on auto physical damage claims jumped 18% in month 3, proactively scheduled a workflow optimization session before the carrier noticed the trend. Resolved a queue routing configuration issue that would have become a major complaint at the next QBR.

## SECTION 5: People & Stakeholder Management

Maintain a deliberate Stakeholder Engagement Map for every strategic account — a living document that captures each stakeholder's role, priorities, communication preferences, and current sentiment.

- C-Suite (CIO, COO, CFO): Quarterly Business Reviews focused on business outcomes, value realization, and strategic roadmap. Communication is concise, visual, and outcome-oriented.
- VP/Director level: Monthly or bi-weekly working sessions focused on program governance, upcoming decisions, and resource alignment
- Project Managers and BAs: Weekly standups, shared project tools (JIRA/Confluence), and daily accessibility via Slack/Teams

*Multi-stakeholder Example:* On a Guidewire PolicyCenter implementation for a personal lines carrier, managing relationships with the CIO (strategic alignment), VP of IT (delivery oversight), Director of Personal Lines (business acceptance), and 3 PMs (daily delivery). The CIO wanted to see loss ratio impact data in QBRs. The VP of IT wanted SLA metrics. The Director of Personal Lines wanted to know if agents were happy with the new submission experience. Customized every touchpoint to the audience — same facts, different framing.

**Q22. Describe a time you had to influence a decision at the client's senior leadership level without formal authority.**

A personal lines carrier's CIO had decided to implement Guidewire PolicyCenter as a big-bang replacement — all lines of business simultaneously. The delivery team assessed this as extremely high risk, but I had no authority to override the client's decision.

Influence approach:
- Built the risk case with data: pulled industry data on big-bang vs. phased Guidewire implementations, showed that phased approaches had 60% higher on-time delivery rates
- Engaged the CFO (not just the CIO): Reframed the conversation around capital risk — a big-bang delay would impact 24 months of IT budget planning
- Presented a phased alternative that preserved their timeline goal: personal auto first (6 months), homeowners second (4 months), specialty lines third (4 months) — net same timeline, dramatically lower risk
- Brought in a Guidewire reference client who had been through a similar decision and chosen phased

*Outcome:* The CIO agreed to a phased approach after the CFO conversation shifted the framing from 'delivery preference' to 'financial risk management.' The personal auto go-live was delivered on time. The CIO later said: 'You were right, and I'm glad you pushed.'

**Q23. How do you mentor and develop junior client services team members?**

Mentorship works best when it's structured and tied to real client situations — not classroom training. Three-component development model:

- Shadowing with debrief: Junior CSMs join client calls for 90 days, then debrief for 20 minutes after every call — what did I pick up on, what questions did I ask, what was I watching for
- Stretch assignments: Give junior CSMs ownership of a specific client workstream (e.g., owning the QBR deck, leading the onboarding for a new Guidewire module) with coaching guardrails
- Domain investment: Require every CSM on insurance accounts to complete Guidewire's product certification for the platform we're delivering — this builds credibility in client conversations

*Development Story:* A junior CSM on my team was managing a personal lines carrier's Guidewire BillingCenter post-go-live account. She was reactive — only calling when clients called. Coached her to run her first proactive QBR, build her own health score for the account, and identify one expansion opportunity. Six months later, she brought in a $450K optimization engagement independently.

## SECTION 6: Behavioral / Situational (STAR Format)

**Q24. Tell me about a time you turned a dissatisfied client into a long-term champion.**

*Situation:* A homeowners insurance carrier was 6 months post-go-live on Guidewire PolicyCenter and had filed a formal complaint with EY's leadership. Their new business issuance process had a 12% error rate — agents were submitting incomplete applications that PolicyCenter was incorrectly accepting, creating rework downstream.

*Task:* Restore the relationship, solve the technical problem, and prevent churn on a $1.8M annual account.

*Action:*
- Flew on-site within 48 hours and ran a structured listening session — no defensiveness, just diagnosis
- Identified that the validation rules in PolicyCenter's personal lines product model were not configured to match the carrier's underwriting guidelines — a configuration gap, not a platform defect
- Assembled a tiger team: 1 PolicyCenter senior configurator + 1 business analyst with homeowners UW experience
- Rebuilt 23 validation rules over 3 weeks in UAT, then ran a joint validation session with the carrier's underwriting team before re-deploying
- Implemented a monthly 'System Quality Review' post-fix to catch any drift

*Result:* Error rate dropped from 12% to 0.8% within 45 days. The carrier's Chief Underwriting Officer — who had been the loudest critic — became a reference client for EY's personal lines practice. They expanded to a Guidewire BillingCenter implementation 8 months later.
