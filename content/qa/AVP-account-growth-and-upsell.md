# Account Growth & Upsell — HM Interview Prep

## Why this matters at AVP level

The HM is hiring a revenue-generating leader, not just a delivery manager. At AVP, you are expected to:
- Expand existing accounts (land and expand)
- Identify upsell opportunities during delivery
- Build the carrier relationship so VM wins the next engagement
- Bring new logos through your network

Every answer in this section should connect delivery excellence → trust → commercial expansion.

---

## Q1: How have you grown an account beyond its original scope?

**What they're really asking:** Can you identify expansion opportunities while you're heads-down on delivery? Do you have commercial instincts, or do you only execute what's in front of you?

**How to answer:**

> "Account growth at this level happens when the client trusts you enough to bring you their next problem before it goes to RFP. That trust is earned during delivery.
>
> A specific example: at EY, we were engaged by Nationwide Personal Lines for a PolicyCenter implementation — personal auto and personal property. That was the original scope. During PI planning in the second release, I noticed their billing team was struggling with reconciliation errors between PolicyCenter and their legacy BillingCenter. It wasn't our scope, but I asked for a 30-minute working session with their billing IT lead.
>
> I came back with a one-pager showing the root cause and a proposed integration fix. I didn't bill for that analysis — I treated it as an investment. Within 60 days, that turned into a BillingCenter modernization engagement. We went from a single-system implementation to a full PolicyCenter + BillingCenter program. The engagement grew by roughly 40% in contract value.
>
> The principle I operate on: in delivery you have the best possible view of the client's problems. If you only execute your statement of work and ignore everything else you see, you're leaving both value and revenue on the table."

---

## Q2: What is your approach to transitioning a project delivery engagement into a long-term managed services or AMS relationship?

**What they're really asking:** Can you convert a one-time implementation into recurring revenue? This is how VM builds long-term carrier relationships.

**How to answer:**

> "I start the AMS conversation at kickoff, not at go-live. The reason: if you wait until go-live to propose managed services, it looks like a last-minute upsell. If you frame it from day one as 'here's how we support you after launch,' it becomes part of the delivery narrative.
>
> My transition playbook has three parts:
>
> **1. Build AMS artifacts during implementation.** Runbooks, support playbooks, incident response procedures — I require these to be authored by the delivery team during the project. By go-live, the AMS team has a complete operating manual, not a knowledge transfer crisis.
>
> **2. Introduce the AMS lead to the client before go-live.** The client should know the person who will own their support relationship. I do a formal AMS introduction 60 days before go-live, structured as a capability briefing — here's our team, here's our SLA model, here's how incidents get handled.
>
> **3. Propose a 90-day hypercare period.** This is a bridge between project delivery and steady-state AMS. It's lower-risk for the carrier to say yes to 90 days of enhanced support than to a 3-year contract. Once you're in hypercare and delivering well, the multi-year AMS contract is a formality.
>
> Using this model, I've converted three out of three implementation engagements into AMS relationships. AMS is also the stickiest revenue in this business — carriers rarely change their managed services provider mid-contract."

## Q3: How do you identify upsell and cross-sell opportunities without seeming opportunistic to the client?

**What they're really asking:** Do you have the maturity to grow accounts without damaging the trust relationship?

**How to answer:**

> "The line between a trusted advisor and an opportunistic vendor is whether you lead with the client's interest or your own.
>
> My rule: I only bring up additional work if I genuinely believe the client needs it and we're the right firm to deliver it. If I'm not confident in both, I don't pitch it.
>
> Practically, I do a quarterly business review with every active client — not a project status call, a strategic review. The agenda is: what's working, what's not, and what's coming in the next 12 months on their roadmap. I listen for: upcoming regulatory changes they haven't planned for, technical debt they've accepted, new business lines they want to support, and leadership changes that create new priorities.
>
> That QBR is where opportunities surface naturally — because I'm asking about their problems, not selling my solutions. When I do propose something, I frame it as: 'Based on what you told me about your priority for commercial lines expansion, here's how we'd approach that.' It lands differently than 'here's our next offering.'
>
> The carrier should feel like you're helping them think, not selling to them. When that's true, they bring you into conversations before they formally go to market — and that's the only position worth being in."

## Q4: Tell me about a time you lost a renewal or failed to grow an account. What did you learn?

**What they're really asking:** Self-awareness and learning agility. Everyone has a loss — how you handle it matters more than the loss itself.

**How to answer:**

> "Early in my delivery management career at EY, we delivered a portal modernization for a mid-size carrier. Technically, it was a solid delivery — on time, within budget, good quality. But at renewal, the client went to a competitor.
>
> When I did the post-mortem, the issue was clear: I had managed the IT relationship well but neglected the business-side stakeholders. The carrier's Chief Claims Officer had been championing a specific self-service feature that we had de-prioritized in the final release. She wasn't vocal about her frustration during delivery, but when renewal came up, she had an opinion.
>
> The lesson: delivery satisfaction and stakeholder satisfaction are not the same thing. Since then, I map every engagement's stakeholder landscape explicitly — IT owner, business owner, executive sponsor, and end-user champion — and I manage a relationship with each one, not just the technical lead.
>
> I also run a formal post-go-live stakeholder survey at 90 days. Not NPS — specific questions: did we deliver what you needed, were you kept informed, do you trust us to deliver the next phase? That gives me signal early enough to act on it."

## Q5: How do you manage a situation where a client wants to expand scope but the commercial model doesn't support it?

**What they're really asking:** Commercial discipline. Can you protect margin while keeping the client relationship intact?

**How to answer:**

> "Scope expansion without commercial adjustment is how engagements erode margin and burn out teams. I've seen firms say yes to everything to keep the client happy and end up delivering at a loss.
>
> My approach is what I call 'transparent value exchange.' When a client brings a scope request, I respond with: 'Here's what that involves, here's the impact on timeline and team, and here's what it would take to absorb it.' I give them three options:
>
> **Option 1:** Fund the scope expansion — here's the change order estimate.
> **Option 2:** Trade it — we deprioritize something of equivalent effort in the current scope.
> **Option 3:** Phase it — it goes into the next release with its own commercial basis.
>
> Most clients respect this framing because it treats them as partners making real tradeoffs, not adversaries to negotiate against. The ones who don't — who push for free scope — are usually reacting to an internal budget pressure, and I try to understand that context before I dig in.
>
> What I never do: absorb scope silently, then deliver less than promised because the team ran out of capacity. That's the worst outcome for everyone."

- "I only pitch what I'd buy if I were the carrier."
- "My QBR agenda starts with their problems, not our capabilities."
