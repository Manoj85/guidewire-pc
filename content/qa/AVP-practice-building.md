# Practice Building, Thought Leadership & Talent Strategy — HM Interview Prep

**Why this section exists:** An AVP at ValueMomentum is not just a delivery manager — you are expected to help build the Guidewire practice: grow the team, develop IP, win deals, and put VM on the map as a thought leader. This is where you differentiate from a project manager who just executes.

---

## Q1: How would you approach building out ValueMomentum's Guidewire delivery practice in your first year?

**What they're really asking:** Do you think like a practice leader or just a delivery manager? Can you grow capability, not just execute programs?

**How to answer:**
> "I'd approach year one in three tracks running simultaneously: people, IP, and market presence.
>
> **People:** The foundation of a strong practice is a certified, specialized team. First 90 days, I'd conduct a skills assessment across every engineer and architect in the GW practice — Guidewire Studio proficiency, Gosu depth, cloud migration experience, and integration expertise. From that, I'd build a certification roadmap: who needs ACE (Analyst Certification Exam), who's ready for associate-level GW certs, who should be in the cloud specialization track. I'd dedicate 10% of billable time per quarter to certification and skill development — that's a protected investment, not optional.
>
> **IP:** At EY, I built reusable accelerators — a rating comparison engine, a SmartCOMM document template library, a MuleSoft integration framework for Guidewire APIs. These cut 20–30% off implementation estimates on subsequent engagements. At VM, I'd inventory what IP already exists in iFoundry and identify the gaps. I'd direct delivery teams to build accelerators as a project deliverable — not as a post-project activity that never happens.
>
> **Market presence:** I'd author two thought leadership pieces in year one — one on Guidewire Cloud migration strategy for mid-market carriers, one on GenAI in claims processing. Both drawing from real delivery experience. I'd submit to Guidewire Connections as a speaker. I'd ask to participate in three carrier discovery conversations where VM is competing for work — as observer first, then lead. Building pipeline presence is part of the AVP role, not separate from it."

## Q2: How do you recruit and retain top Guidewire talent in a market where everyone is competing for the same people?

**How to answer:**
> "Guidewire-certified developers and architects are genuinely scarce. The carriers know it, the SIs know it, and the candidates know it. You cannot win on compensation alone.
>
> What actually retains Guidewire practitioners is: interesting work, investment in growth, and a clear career path.
>
> **Interesting work:** A developer who has done one PolicyCenter implementation wants the next engagement to be different — a different line of business, a cloud migration, a GenAI integration. I deliberately rotate high-performers across engagements so they're not doing the same thing for three years. Repetition is the enemy of retention in this market.
>
> **Investment:** At EY, I built a Guidewire internal certification program — structured curriculum, study groups, exam funding, and recognition. Engineers who knew their growth was being invested in stayed. Engineers who felt like billable resources on a treadmill left.
>
> **Career path:** I make the path visible and explicit. Here's what Associate Developer looks like, here's what gets you to Lead Architect, here's what gets you to Delivery Manager. I don't let the path be ambiguous — ambiguity about growth is the second most common reason good people leave.
>
> For recruiting: the best source is referrals from current team members. A team that feels well-led will bring in people from their network. I run structured referral conversations — not just 'refer someone,' but 'here's who specifically I'm looking for, here's what makes VM a good move for a GW practitioner.'"

## Q3: How do you build reusable delivery IP during a client engagement without the client objecting that you're building assets on their dime?

**What they're really asking:** Commercial sophistication — can you grow the firm's capability without creating client conflict?

**How to answer:**
> "This is a real tension and it has to be managed transparently, not avoided.
>
> My approach: anything built to solve a client-specific problem belongs to the client — their product model, their Gosu rules, their data migration scripts. But the framework, the pattern, and the approach belong to the firm. I make this distinction explicit in the SOW and I revisit it if the client ever raises it.
>
> A concrete example: at CompSource Mutual, we built a ClaimCenter integration framework for their payment processor. The specific mappings and business rules were theirs. But the MuleSoft connector pattern, the error handling framework, and the retry logic — those were reusable patterns I had my team document as an internal accelerator. We used the same framework on the Canal Insurance engagement six months later, which cut our integration estimate by 35%.
>
> The client benefit is actually real here: when your team has reusable patterns, they build faster and with fewer defects. That's a better outcome for the client, not worse. I frame it that way: 'We're applying proven patterns from prior engagements, which means faster delivery and fewer integration surprises for you.' That framing changes the conversation entirely."

## Q4: Describe a time you created and sold a new service offering or capability to a client.

**How to answer (STAR):**
> "**Situation:** In 2024 at EY, GenAI was generating enormous interest from insurance carrier executives but very little production-grade implementation. Most conversations were theoretical.
>
> **Task:** I saw an opportunity to turn our GenAI experimentation into a productized offering — a structured engagement that took a carrier from zero to a working GenAI pilot in 12 weeks.
>
> **Action:** I designed a three-phase offering: a 4-week discovery and use case identification (which claims or underwriting workflows had the most automation opportunity), a 6-week RAG pipeline build using LangChain on Azure OpenAI, and a 2-week carrier UAT and handoff. I priced it as a fixed-fee engagement — carriers were uncomfortable with T&M for something this new.
>
> I took the offering to two existing carrier clients where I had executive relationships. The pitch was simple: 'We will build a working GenAI model on your actual data — claims documents, policy files — that your team can demo to your board in 12 weeks. Fixed price, no surprises.' Both carriers said yes within 30 days.
>
> **Result:** We ran both POCs, one for claims intake automation (FNOL document extraction) and one for commercial underwriting submission summarization. The claims intake pilot showed a 50% reduction in manual data entry. One of the clients funded a full production build immediately. The other is in our pipeline for Q2.
>
> The lesson: new capabilities sell when you remove the ambiguity. Fixed scope, fixed price, real working output. That's what converts 'interesting' into 'approved.'"

## Q5: How do you win a deal against a larger competitor like Accenture or Deloitte?

**What they're really asking:** Can you articulate VM's differentiator credibly — and do you believe it yourself?

**How to answer:**
> "I've been on both sides of this — at EY competing against VM, and now I want to be the firm that wins against EY.
>
> The honest answer is: large SIs win on brand recognition, global delivery scale, and relationships at the CIO level. Those are real advantages. But they lose on depth, speed, and accountability.
>
> When I competed against VM at EY, the deals we lost were the ones where the carrier specifically wanted a team that lived and breathed insurance — not consultants who rotated through insurance between banking and retail projects. VM's team is 100% P&C. That's a genuine differentiator.
>
> The way I'd win against a Big 4 in a Guidewire opportunity: lead with client references from similar carriers. If the opportunity is a mid-size WC carrier, I want a CompSource Mutual-level reference on the table in the first meeting. Lead with specificity — I can name their exact line of business, their regulatory environment, their likely integration footprint. Generalists can't do that without research.
>
> Then win on the team. Show the carrier the actual people who will deliver — certified GW architects, delivery managers who have run WC ClaimCenter programs, not junior consultants supervised by a partner who shows up at steering committees. Carriers have been burned by bait-and-switch. The team I present is the team that delivers.
>
> And win on commercial structure. VM can be more flexible on pricing than a Big 4 because the overhead model is different. I'd use that to offer a fixed-fee pilot phase that a larger firm won't touch."

## Q7: How do you handle a team member who is technically strong but struggling to work with clients?

**How to answer:**
> "Technical talent without client skills is a ceiling problem — the person will plateau at a level below where they could otherwise go, and it limits what you can put in front of clients.
>
> I address it directly and specifically. Not 'you need to work on your communication' — that's too vague to act on. I identify the specific behavior: interrupting in client calls, using technical jargon in business conversations, going quiet when challenged.
>
> My approach: I pair them with a strong client-facing senior for 60 days with an explicit development goal. I debrief after every client interaction — what went well, what landed differently than intended, what they'd do differently. I give them low-stakes client exposure first (a status update, a technical walkthrough) before high-stakes (a steering committee, a difficult conversation).
>
> On the Nationwide program, I had a senior architect who was exceptional at Guidewire product model design but would visibly shut down in client workshops when business stakeholders pushed back. We worked on it for two months — explicit coaching, roleplay before workshops, and I gave him a 'rescue signal' where I'd step in if a conversation was going sideways. By the end of the program, he was running workshops independently. He's now one of my strongest client-facing architects.
>
> The investment is worth it. Losing a technically strong person because they hit a client skills ceiling is a waste. Developing them into a full practitioner creates a long-term asset for the firm."
