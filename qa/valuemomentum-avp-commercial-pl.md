# Commercial & P&L Management — HM Interview Prep

## Why this matters at AVP level

At AVP, you are accountable for the financial health of your portfolio — not just delivery outcomes. The HM will probe:
- Do you understand margin, utilization, and blended rate?
- Can you manage a P&L, not just a project plan?
- How do you handle commercial pressure from clients without destroying margin?
- Can you price a deal and defend it?

Own this territory confidently. Most delivery-focused candidates go soft here — it's your differentiator.

---

## Q1: Have you had P&L accountability? How did you manage it?

**What they're really asking:** Are you a delivery manager or a business leader? Do you understand revenue, cost, and margin — not just schedules and defects?

**How to answer:**

> "Yes — at EY I've had direct P&L accountability on my engagement portfolio. At the Senior Manager level, I own the commercial relationship: from deal estimation through delivery margin management and AMS renewal.
>
> My P&L levers are:
>
> **Revenue:** I track contract value vs. recognized revenue monthly. Any variance — scope changes, time and materials overruns, deferred milestones — gets flagged immediately because it affects our revenue recognition.
>
> **Cost:** The biggest cost driver in delivery is utilization. I track weekly utilization by resource across my portfolio. My target is 80–85% billable utilization — below that is waste, above 90% for sustained periods leads to burnout and attrition.
>
> **Margin:** I review margin by project, not just portfolio. A project that looks green at the portfolio level can be masking a loss-maker that a good one is subsidizing. I want to know which engagements are healthy and which need intervention.
>
> The discipline I apply: if an engagement is trending toward margin erosion, I address it early — either through a scope conversation with the client, a rate adjustment at renewal, or a staffing model change. I don't let a margin problem compound for two quarters before acting."

## Q2: How do you estimate and price a Guidewire implementation? Walk me through your approach.

**What they're really asking:** Can you build a credible deal, defend it to the client, and deliver to it? This is a core AVP competency.

**How to answer:**

> "My estimation model for a Guidewire implementation has five components:
>
> **1. Scope decomposition:** I break the work into workstreams — product model configuration, Gosu customization, integration layer, data migration, portal/UI, testing, and release management. Each workstream gets its own estimate.
>
> **2. Complexity factors:** Not all PolicyCenter implementations are equal. I apply complexity multipliers based on: number of lines of business, number of rating plans, number of integrations (each external system adds 15–20% integration cost), data migration volume, and carrier's existing technical debt.
>
> **3. Team model:** I define the role breakdown — solution architect, lead developer, mid developers, QA lead, QA engineers, integration engineers, delivery manager, BA. Onshore vs. offshore ratio directly affects blended rate and therefore price. A typical GW implementation I'd structure 30% onshore (senior roles, client-facing) and 70% offshore (execution).
>
> **4. Risk buffer:** I add 15–20% for a complex GW implementation, 10% for a simpler one. This covers unknown unknowns — undocumented business rules, data quality issues in migration, integration surprises.
>
> **5. Phasing:** I always propose the implementation in phases — typically a pilot/MVP phase and a full rollout phase. This lowers the carrier's initial commitment risk and gives us a natural renewal point.
>
> I validate my estimate against benchmark data from prior engagements — cost per line of business, cost per integration point, hours per Gosu customization. If my bottom-up estimate is significantly outside benchmarks, I investigate why before I present it."

## Q3: How do you handle a situation where your engagement is tracking over budget?

**What they're really asking:** Do you panic, hide it, or manage it? This is a leadership maturity question.

**How to answer:**

> "First rule: no surprises to the client or to leadership. If an engagement is tracking over budget, the worst thing I can do is wait until we've blown past it to have the conversation.
>
> My process:
>
> **Diagnose fast.** I want to know within the first week of a budget variance signal whether it's a one-time spike or a trend. Is it a scope issue, a productivity issue, or an estimation error?
>
> **If it's a scope issue** — something the client asked for that wasn't in the original SOW — I document it immediately as a change request. I don't absorb undefined scope. The client has to make a decision: fund it, defer it, or trade it for something else.
>
> **If it's a productivity issue** — the team is slower than estimated — I diagnose the cause. Is it a technical blocker? A resourcing gap? A process inefficiency? I fix the root cause, not the symptom.
>
> **If it's an estimation error** — I underestimated the work — I own it. I go to the client with a clear explanation: here's what we estimated, here's what we found, here's what we need. I don't spin it. Carriers respect honesty, and they've seen enough projects to know estimations aren't perfect.
>
> The conversation I never want to have is: 'We've been over budget for 6 weeks and we didn't tell you.' That conversation ends careers and relationships."

## Q4: How do you manage resource utilization and bench cost in a services business?

**What they're really asking:** Do you understand the economics of a professional services firm? Bench cost is one of the biggest margin killers.

**How to answer:**

> "Bench cost is a real P&L risk in services. Every week a senior engineer sits unbilled is margin gone. I manage it proactively on two horizons:
>
> **Short-term (0–4 weeks):** I maintain a portfolio-level view of who is rolling off and when. If someone is coming off a project in 3 weeks and I don't have their next engagement confirmed, I start working the pipeline immediately — internal projects, pre-sales support, training initiatives. I use the bench productively; I don't let it be invisible.
>
> **Long-term (1–3 months):** I keep close alignment between my delivery pipeline and my hiring plan. I'd rather slightly understaffed on new work and use contractors to flex up than overbuilt a permanent team that goes on bench.
>
> One lever I use in Guidewire delivery specifically: AMS tail engagements. Once a carrier goes live, I keep a small core team on AMS — 2–3 people. That creates a buffer. If a new engagement starts slow, I can temporarily pull from the AMS team and backfill. It reduces the cliff-edge transitions that create bench spikes.
>
> At EY, I maintained portfolio utilization above 82% consistently — by managing transitions, not just delivery."

## Q5: How do you approach contract negotiations with a carrier — specifically around T&M vs. fixed-price?

**What they're really asking:** Do you understand contractual risk? Can you negotiate without losing the deal?

**How to answer:**

> "The commercial structure should reflect the risk allocation — and that requires honesty about what's known and unknown at the time of contracting.
>
> **Fixed-price** works when scope is well-defined, requirements are stable, and we have prior data from similar engagements. A standard PolicyCenter implementation with a defined product model and a known integration list — I'll price that fixed-fee. It's clean for the carrier and drives delivery discipline on our side.
>
> **T&M** works when requirements are evolving — a transformation that starts with discovery, or a greenfield engagement where we're defining architecture and product model together. Putting a fixed price on undefined scope is how you create adversarial relationships. Someone always loses, and usually it's the delivery firm.
>
> **My preferred model** for most Guidewire implementations is a hybrid: a fixed-fee for each defined phase with T&M flexibility within a capped band for scope variations. The carrier gets budget predictability; we retain protection against scope drift.
>
> In negotiations, I focus on the risk conversation rather than the price conversation. If the carrier wants fixed-price on poorly defined scope, my response is: 'We can do fixed-price if we invest 4 weeks in a discovery and scope baseline first — that de-risks the number for both of us.' That's usually a yes, because it's reasonable."

## Q6: How do you think about account profitability vs. strategic value? Would you ever take a loss-leader engagement?

**What they're really asking:** Do you have strategic judgment, or are you purely a margin manager?

**How to answer:**

> "I'd take a loss-leader for the right reasons — and there are two scenarios where I think it's justified:
>
> **New logo entry:** If a carrier is a strategic target — significant long-term AMS potential, reference value in a key market segment, or a named account that opens other doors — I'd accept thinner margin on the initial engagement. But I'd go in with eyes open: a documented strategic rationale, a path to margin improvement at renewal, and a ceiling on how much we'd subsidize.
>
> **Capability investment:** If an engagement lets us build a reusable accelerator, a new practice capability, or a GenAI POC that we can take to market — I'd accept lower margin in exchange for the IP we develop. That's an R&D investment disguised as a delivery engagement.
>
> What I won't do: take unprofitable work just to keep people busy or to avoid a difficult pipeline conversation. That's how firms get into a pattern of low-margin work that's hard to escape.
>
> The discipline is: every sub-margin engagement needs a formal write-up — here's why we're doing it, here's what we expect in return, here's the decision if it doesn't materialize. Otherwise 'strategic' becomes a word we use to justify bad deals."

## COMMERCIAL VOCABULARY — USE THESE TERMS NATURALLY

| Term | Context |
|---|---|
| **Blended rate** | Onshore/offshore average billing rate across the team |
| **Utilization** | % of team hours that are billable; target 80–85% |
| **Realization** | % of billed hours actually collected; matters in T&M |
| **SOW** | Statement of Work — the contractual scope boundary |
| **Change order / RFC** | Formal mechanism for approved scope changes |
| **Margin** | Revenue minus cost; track per engagement, not just portfolio |
| **Land and expand** | Win initial engagement, grow account organically |
| **AMS / managed services** | Recurring post-go-live revenue — stickiest commercial model |
| **Fixed-fee vs. T&M** | Two primary commercial structures; hybrid is common |
| **Bench** | Unbilled team members between engagements — direct cost |
| **Pipeline** | Forward-looking revenue opportunities in pre-sales |
